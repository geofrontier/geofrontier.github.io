What is Movecraft?

Movecraft transforms static builds into moving ships. Instead of using minecarts or boats, you construct a vessel out of legitimate blocks, place a few specialized signs, and then sail it across the ocean. The ship moves as a single unit, with all players aboard moving with it.

The system is designed for the Age of Exploration. Ships are slow, require careful handling, and are vulnerable to storms, sea creatures, and enemy cannon fire. A poorly built ship will sink. A well built ship becomes your home on the waves.

How Ships Work

Ship Identification

Every ship is defined by its craft type sign. This single sign, placed anywhere on the vessel, tells Movecraft what class of ship you have built. The first line must be the ship type, for example "Brigantine" or "Pinnace". Without this sign, your build is just a build, not a ship.

Ship Movement

Ships move by teleporting forward in a series of jumps, not by smooth sailing. The distance traveled per jump, called cruise skip, varies by ship class. When you command your ship to move, the game checks if the entire ship would fit in the new position. If any part would intersect solid land, another ship, or major structures, the movement fails and your ship stays put.

If you do hit something, your ship may take damage and you will need to back up. To reverse direction, turn your ship around using the helm sign and sail back the way you came. Repeated collisions can severely damage your vessel.

Storms and Environmental Damage

Storms at Sea

The oceans are not safe. Storms can roll in without warning, damaging ships caught in open water. During a storm, blocks on your ship may randomly break, especially sails and upper structures. If you see dark clouds gathering or hear thunder, return to port or seek shelter. There is no way to stop storm damage except to not be at sea.

Damage from storms must be repaired manually by replacing broken blocks. Always carry spare planks and wool.

Ownership and Access

Any player can pilot any ship by default. To restrict access, use a pilot sign listing authorized pilots. The ship's owner is the player who placed the craft type sign. This player can add or remove pilots via the sign.

How to Pilot a Ship

Becoming the Pilot

Before you can control a ship, you must become its active pilot. Right click on the craft type sign. You will see a message confirming that you are now piloting the vessel. Only one player can pilot at a time. If someone else is already piloting, you will need to wait or ask them to step down.

Basic Controls

  • Moving Forward: Hold a stick in your hand and right click in the direction you want to sail. The ship will move forward in the direction you are facing. This works for forward movement, but many pilots prefer using the helm and cruise signs for long journeys.
  • Turning: Find your helm sign, labeled [helm]. Right click it to rotate the ship 90 degrees clockwise. Left click to rotate 90 degrees counter clockwise. Turn first, then sail forward.
  • Sailing Diagonally or Backwards: The stick method works for any direction. Face the angle you want to travel, hold the stick, and right click. The ship will move in that exact direction. This is useful for maneuvering in tight spaces or backing away from obstacles.
  • Cruise Mode: If you have a cruise sign, right click it to toggle cruise on and off. When cruise is ON, the ship will continue moving forward automatically without needing to click repeatedly. Be careful. Cruise mode does not avoid obstacles.
  • Stopping: Right click a release sign if you have one, or type /release in chat. The ship will stop moving immediately.

Piloting Tips

  • Always check your surroundings before moving. A ship stuck inside land takes damage and may sink.
  • Use cruise mode only in open water. If you hit something while cruising, you will take damage before you can react.
  • The stick method is best for precise movements like docking or backing up. The helm plus cruise is better for long distance travel.
  • If you fall off your ship, type /manoverboard to teleport back to your manoverboard sign. Always place one.

How to Build a Ship: Step by Step

Step 1: Choose Your Ship Class

Decide what you need, a fast trader like a Pinnace, a warship like a Brigantine, or a flagship like a Man o War. Each class has specific size requirements, as shown in the Ship Classes table below.

Step 2: Gather Materials

You will need planks, 30 to 100 percent of total volume, and wool for sails, 4 to 20 percent. Naval vessels can use up to 10 percent iron blocks for cannons. Your ship must be at least 30 percent planks, or it will not be recognized.

Step 3: Build the Hull

Construct your ship within the size range for your chosen class. The hull must be solidly connected. All blocks count toward the ship's total volume. You can leave interior spaces hollow. Only the blocks themselves matter, not the air inside.

Step 4: Add Sails Using Wool

Wool blocks represent sails. They must make up 4 to 20 percent of your total block count. You can place them traditionally as masts and sails, but they count as part of the ship structure.

Step 5: Place the Required Sign

Place a sign with the ship class on the first line, for example "Brigantine". This sign can be anywhere on the hull, mast, or deck. Without this, your build will not move.

Step 6: Add Optional Signs

Add helm signs for steering, cruise signs for continuous movement, pilot signs for access control, and manoverboard signs for easy return. These make the ship usable but are not strictly required.

Step 7: Test Your Ship

Right click the craft type sign to become pilot. Hold a stick, face the direction you want to go, and right click. If everything is correct, your ship will move forward. If not, check your material percentages and sign placement.

Experienced Sailor Recommendations

Secure Your Ship When Docked

Always dock your ship inside a claimed chunk. Place a piston against the hull and extend it into the ship. This physically prevents theft. Even if someone places their own pilot sign, the ship will not move while blocked by the piston. Retract the piston when you are ready to sail.

Reinforce Critical Sections

Storms and cannon fire do not destroy blocks uniformly. Reinforce your hull with multiple layers around vital areas, especially near the waterline. If the outer layer gets blown away, you have backups. Obsidian is excellent for core structural elements.

Always Set a Manoverboard Sign

Falling off your ship in open ocean is a death sentence. Place a Manoverboard sign somewhere easily accessible. If you fall, type /manoverboard and you will teleport back to that sign. Do not learn this lesson the hard way.

Carry Repair Materials

Always have spare planks and wool in your inventory. If your ship takes damage mid voyage, you can repair it immediately by placing new blocks. There is no repair command. Waiting until you reach port might be too late.

Use Pilot Signs for Crew Ships

For faction warships, list all trusted crew members on a pilot sign. This prevents enemies who board your ship from stealing it, but still allows your crew to take the helm if the captain falls.

Why My Ship Won't Move

You built a beautiful ship, placed your signs, right clicked with a stick, and nothing happened. Don't panic. This is the most common experience for new shipwrights. Work through these checks in order.

1

Did you right click the craft type sign first?

You must become the pilot by right clicking the craft type sign. Until you do, the ship will not respond to controls.

2

Are you standing on the ship?

You must be physically on the ship's blocks. Standing next to it or on nearby terrain won't work. The game needs to know which craft you're trying to move.

3

Are you holding a stick?

The movement item is a stick. Not a sword, not a fishing rod, not your bare hand. A regular stick, in your main hand, right clicked in the direction you want to sail.

4

Does it have a Craft Type sign?

Check for the required sign. First line must be exactly the ship class name, for example "Brigantine" or "Pinnace". No extra text, no punctuation. Just the class name.

5

Is it within block limits?

Count your blocks. Every ship class has a minimum and maximum size. Too small or too large, and the ship won't be recognized. Check the Ship Classes table for your vessel's required range.

6

Are material percentages correct?

Your ship needs at least 30 percent planks and 4 to 20 percent wool. If you built with too much stone or too little wool, the ship will sit dead in the water.

7

Is cruise already enabled?

If you have a cruise sign and it's set to ON, the ship may already be trying to move. Toggle it OFF, then try the stick again.

8

Is it intersecting terrain?

Ships cannot phase through land, other ships, or major structures. If any part of your ship would clip into something, movement fails. Try moving to open water.

9

Is someone else piloting?

Only one player can control a ship at a time. If another player is currently piloting, you will need to wait or ask them to release control.

10

Does it have forbidden blocks?

Check your build for pistons, redstone components, or other prohibited materials. One forbidden block can disable the entire ship.

Still stuck? Ask in Discord with a screenshot of your ship and a player online can usually spot the issue in seconds. Nine times out of ten, it is a missing craft type sign or a block count problem.

Material Composition Requirements

Every ship must meet these material percentages to function.

Planks

30% to 100%

At least 30 percent of your ship's total blocks must be any type of planks. This is the primary structural material.

Wool for Sails

4% to 20%

Wool blocks represent sails. You need enough to catch wind, but too much makes the ship unstable.

The remaining percentage can be any combination of allowed materials, including stone, glass, and wood variants.

Forbidden Blocks

Do not use these on any ship: Pistons, Sticky Pistons, Slime Blocks, Honey Blocks, Observers, Dispensers, Droppers, Redstone components including wire, blocks, repeaters, and comparators, Daylight Detectors, Beacons, and Ender Chests. These are disabled for Age of Exploration authenticity and to prevent exploits.

Recommended Progression

New to ships? Start small and work your way up.

  1. Pinnace or Cutter — Learn the basics of building and sailing. These are cheap, fast, and forgiving.
  2. Schooner or Caravel — Your first warship. Learn cannon mechanics and naval combat.
  3. Brigantine or Corsair — Serious raiding and privateering. Heavier firepower and more crew.
  4. Frigate or Galleon — Fleet operations and valuable cargo runs.
  5. Man o War — End game flagship. Requires a nation's worth of resources and crew.
