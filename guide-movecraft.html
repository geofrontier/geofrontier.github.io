Movecraft Naval System

A Complete Guide to Building, Sailing, and Fighting with Ships

What is Movecraft?

Movecraft transforms static builds into moving ships. Instead of using minecarts or boats, you construct a vessel out of legitimate blocks, place a few specialized signs, and then command it to sail across the ocean. The ship moves as a single unit, with all players aboard moving with it.

The system is designed for the Age of Exploration—ships are slow, require careful handling, and are vulnerable to storms, sea creatures, and enemy cannon fire. A poorly built ship will sink. A well-built ship becomes your home on the waves.

How Ships Work

Ship Identification

Every ship is defined by its craft type sign. This single sign—placed anywhere on the vessel—tells Movecraft what class of ship you've built. The first line must be the ship type (e.g., "Brigantine" or "Pinnace"). Without this sign, your build is just a build, not a ship.

Movement

Ships move in the direction the pilot is facing. When you issue the /craft sail command, the ship teleports forward a set number of blocks (its "cruise skip" distance) in that direction. This isn't smooth sailing—it's a series of jumps. The ship must have a clear path; if any part would intersect solid land or another ship, movement fails.

Controls

You control the ship through a combination of signs and commands. The helm sign allows 90-degree rotations. The cruise sign lets you toggle continuous movement. Most pilots use a mix—rotating with the helm, then sailing forward with commands.

Ship Durability (How Sinking Works)

Contrary to what the numbers might suggest, ships do not have a flat "damage resistance." Instead, they have two critical thresholds:

  • Damage Threshold (80-90%) — When a ship takes enough damage to reach this percentage of its total blocks, it begins to take continuous damage. For most ships, this is 80% (meaning 20% of the ship must be destroyed before it starts deteriorating). The Man-o-War has 90% (only 10% destruction required to trigger sinking).
  • Sink Threshold (60-80%) — This is the percentage of the ship that must be totally destroyed for the ship to sink. For a Man-o-War, 80% of its blocks must be destroyed before it finally goes under. For a Pinnace, only 60% destruction is required.

In plain terms: The Man-o-War starts taking damage earlier (90% threshold) but requires far more total destruction to actually sink (80% sink threshold). Smaller ships stay intact longer but sink faster once they start taking damage.

Damage comes from storms, ramming, and cannon fire. When blocks are destroyed, the ship's integrity decreases. Repair using /craft repair and the appropriate materials.

Piloting and Ownership

Any player can pilot any ship by default. To restrict access, use a pilot sign listing authorized pilots. The ship's "owner" is the player who placed the craft type sign—this player can add or remove pilots via the sign.

How to Build a Ship: Step by Step

Step 1: Choose Your Ship Class

Decide what you need—a fast trader (Pinnace), a warship (Brigantine), or a flagship (Man-o-War). Each class has specific size requirements (see the Ship Classes table below).

Step 2: Gather Materials

You'll need planks (30-100% of total volume), wool for sails (4-20%), and various structural blocks. Naval vessels can use up to 10% iron blocks for cannons. Critical: Your ship must be at least 30% planks, or it won't be recognized.

Step 3: Build the Hull

Construct your ship within the size range for your chosen class. The hull must be solidly connected—all blocks count toward the ship's total volume. Leave interior spaces hollow; only the blocks themselves matter, not the air inside.

Step 4: Add Sails (Wool)

Wool blocks represent sails. They must comprise 4-20% of your total block count. You can place them traditionally as masts and sails, but they count as part of the ship structure.

Step 5: Place the Required Sign

Place a sign with the ship class on the first line (e.g., "Brigantine"). This sign can be anywhere—on the hull, mast, or deck. Without this, your build won't move.

Step 6: Add Optional Signs

Add helm signs for steering, cruise signs for continuous movement, pilot signs for access control, and manoverboard signs for easy return. These make the ship usable but aren't strictly required.

Step 7: Test Your Ship

Stand on the ship, face the direction you want to go, and type /craft sail. If everything is correct, your ship will move forward. If not, check your material percentages and sign placement.

Experienced Sailor Recommendations

Secure Your Ship When Docked

Always dock your ship inside a claimed chunk. Place a piston against the hull and extend it into the ship. This physically prevents theft—even if someone places their own pilot sign, the ship won't move while blocked by the piston. Retract the piston when you're ready to sail.

Reinforce Critical Sections

Storms and cannon fire don't destroy blocks uniformly. Reinforce your hull with multiple layers around vital areas—especially near the waterline. If the outer layer gets blown away, you have backups. Obsidian is excellent for core structural elements.

Always Set a Manoverboard Sign

Falling off your ship in open ocean is a death sentence. Place a "Manoverboard" sign somewhere easily accessible. If you fall, type /manoverboard and you'll teleport back to that sign. Don't learn this lesson the hard way.

Carry Repair Materials

Always have spare planks and wool in your inventory. If your ship takes damage mid-voyage, you can repair it immediately with /craft repair. Waiting until you reach port might be too late.

Use Pilot Signs for Crew Ships

For faction warships, list all trusted crew members on a pilot sign. This prevents enemies who board your ship from stealing it, but still allows your crew to take the helm if the captain falls.

Material Composition Requirements

Every ship must meet these material percentages to function:

Planks

30% - 100%

At least 30% of your ship's total blocks must be any type of planks. This is the primary structural material.

Wool (Sails)

4% - 20%

Wool blocks represent sails. You need enough to catch wind, but too much makes the ship unstable.

The remaining percentage can be any combination of allowed materials (stone, glass, wood variants, etc.).

Forbidden Blocks

Do not use these on any ship: Pistons, Sticky Pistons, Slime Blocks, Honey Blocks, Observers, Dispensers, Droppers, Redstone components (wire, blocks, repeaters, comparators), Daylight Detectors, Beacons, Ender Chests. These are disabled for Age of Exploration authenticity and to prevent exploits.

Recommended Progression

New to ships? Start small and work your way up:

  1. Pinnace or Cutter — Learn the basics of building and sailing. These are cheap, fast, and forgiving.
  2. Schooner or Caravel — Your first warship. Learn cannon mechanics and naval combat.
  3. Brigantine or Corsair — Serious raiding and privateering. Heavier firepower, more crew.
  4. Frigate or Galleon — Fleet operations and valuable cargo runs.
  5. Man-o-War — End-game flagship. Requires a nation's worth of resources and crew.
